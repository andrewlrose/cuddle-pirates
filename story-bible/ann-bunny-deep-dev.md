# Ann Bunny — Deep Character Development

> **Status:** Session 2 development, May 1, 2026.
> Extends `characters.md` profiles without overwriting them.
> This document lives underneath the bible — it holds the decisions the
> writers need to know before episode one without stating them in the show.

---

## The Historical Gap

Anne Bonny's last confirmed moment in the historical record is a courtroom in
Spanish Town, Jamaica, November 1720. Sentence: death. Status: reprieved —
the record doesn't explain why, only that she was with child. After that:
nothing. No death notice. No release record. No ship's manifest. No grave.

The historical record doesn't say she escaped. It doesn't say she didn't.

The show's answer — which is never stated, never confirmed — is that she
entered the Hypnagogic Seas through the one door that was open to her. She
was in exactly the right liminal state at exactly the right moment: not asleep,
not awake, not alive in the way the court expected her to be, not dead in the
way it intended. Between worlds. The Hypnagogic Seas know that state. They
are forgiving of people who arrive in it.

What was she between? A death that didn't happen and a life she couldn't
return to. The woman who had stood on the deck of the William and the woman
she would have been if she'd married a respectable man in Charles Town. The
crew that was hanged and the sentence that wasn't carried out. She was between
all of it — and the Hypnagogic Seas recognized one of their own.

She did not choose to arrive. Once there, she chose to stay.

**What the show will never say:** Anne Bonny walked out of Spanish Town
Prison into a morning that no one else saw, into a stretch of road that
became water before she finished crossing it, and she never looked back
because looking back at that moment was not something she was built to do.

## The Name

**What she was:** Anne Bonny.

**What she is:** Ann Bunny.

**What happened in between:** Three hundred years of children.

She didn't rename herself. The Hypnagogic Seas have their own relationship
with names — they soften them, warm them, find the version that lands gently
on the ear of a child who is almost asleep. "Anne Bonny" in a child's bedroom
sounds like something that happened far away and long ago. "Ann Bunny" sounds
like something that is here now, in this room, on your side.

The first child who couldn't say it right. The first stuffed rabbit somewhere
in the world named after her — the name written on a tag in a child's
handwriting. The centuries of gradual transmission. By the time she noticed
the name had changed, she had already answered to it for decades.

She does not hate the name. She has more complicated feelings, which she does
not discuss. "Anne Bonny" belonged to someone she was in 1720: angry in a
specific way, carrying specific wounds, standing in a specific courtroom
waiting to find out what she was about to become. She is still that woman.
But she is also everything that came after. "Ann Bunny" is the name for
everything that came after.

When she introduces herself to a child, she says "Ann Bunny" without
hesitation. It is the correct name for what she is in this room, doing this
work. When she is alone on the Fluffy Dutchman, watching the Hypnagogic Seas,
she sometimes thinks in the other name. The tail stops twitching in those
moments. It is the only time it stops of its own accord.

**The scene the show is holding:**
Somewhere in the extended roster — a character who knew her before — says
her old name in the middle of a crowded room. The tail goes completely still.
Every crew member notices. No one says anything. The show earned this scene
by never rushing toward it.

**What never to state directly:** The connection between Ann Bunny the First
Mate and Anne Bonny the historical pirate. The audience can find it if they
look. Let the name itself be the clue, sitting in plain sight.

## The Three Centuries — What Accumulates

She arrived in the Hypnagogic Seas with: the skills of a first mate, a
specific kind of anger, and nothing else.

**What she built:**

Everything in her current character profile is something she built. The
stage-director precision. The mission focus. The ability to cut Drake's
monologue with a look after watching it for the hundredth time. The
knowledge of which floorboards creak in a thousand different houses. The
ability to read a child's worry from across the room before a single word
is spoken. The ability to not get attached.

That last one she practices constantly. She fails at it every time.

**What the three centuries cost:**

She knows what comes next for every child she visits. She has seen the arc
enough times to know it completely. Children she has loved have grown up —
not died, just grown — boxed up the stuffed animals, moved out, had children
of their own. She sometimes moves through a room she visited thirty years ago
and the child she knew is now the parent: fully awake, fully adult, unable to
see her at all.

She carries this as knowledge, not as grief. The grief was in the first few
decades, before she learned to carry it. Now it is weather. She walks through
it without remarking on it. The tail twitches a little differently in rooms
she has been in before — a slightly faster rhythm, slightly less even. The
crew has never asked about it.

**What she will not say out loud:**

Every room she returns to costs more than a new room. Not the first visit,
which is all energy and focus and the mission in front of her. The returns.
The rooms she already knows. The children she has started to care about. She
tells herself the heist is the point, the pillow fight is the point. She is
very good at telling herself this, and she has had three hundred years of
practice. She has not yet convinced herself entirely.

## The Real Motivation — Layered Answer

**Layer 1 — What she says:**
The pillow fights at Puff-tooga. She loves them. This is completely true.
The pillow fights are the best thing in the Hypnagogic Seas, she is very
good at them, and they bring her genuine joy that is not performance and
not cover story. She would miss them profoundly if they stopped. Anyone
who dismisses this as "not her real motivation" is wrong. It is one of
her real motivations.

**Layer 2 — What Drake suspects:**
She can't stop. He's watched her between missions — she is not at rest.
She is already scoping the next one. His theory: she is addicted to the
exact moment a child's face changes, when the fear softens and the trust
opens. He's not wrong about what the moment is. He thinks it's a
vulnerability. He's wrong about that part.

**Layer 3 — What the tail knows:**
She keeps coming back because she was once in the position of the child.
Not literally a child — she was in her early twenties when she entered
the Hypnagogic Seas — but she was liminal, terrified, unable to cross
into what came next. The Hypnagogic Seas took her in. No one came to
help her with what she was facing. She figured out where she was and
what she was supposed to do and she did it alone, and it was long, and
she still carries the shape of that aloneness.

Every child she sends to sleep is a child who was not alone in the
liminal moment. That is the real engine. It is not sentimental — it is
structural, rooted in a specific experience she has never described to
anyone and never will. She does this because she knows the alternative.
The alternative has a specific texture and specific weight and she
carries it with her into every room.

**Layer 4 — The season finale answer:**
Why is she still doing this after three hundred years, when most beings
of the Hypnagogic Seas have settled into fixed roles or faded into the
deep waters?

Because she is still waiting for something.

She doesn't know what it is. She knows it has not happened yet. She knows
— not consciously, but in the way you know the shape of something before
you've named it — that it will matter enormously when it does. This is
what the tail has been signaling all along, in every mission, in every
room, in every moment she chose to stay when she could have gone.

She does not know that Junie's room is where that something begins.
She is not yet aware that she should know this.

## Ann Bunny and Sir Fuzzy Drake — The Foundational History

**They did not start as captain and first mate.**

That is the current arrangement and it functions beautifully, but it is
the arrangement they arrived at, not the one they started with.

**Before Drake, she worked alone.**

She was efficient, solitary, and effective by any measure. She didn't
need a crew, didn't need someone to deliver the dramatic monologue while
she ran the actual heist. She had developed her own methods and routes
and her own working relationship with the Hypnagogic Seas. She was, by
any objective assessment, already very good at this.

**Drake found her.**

He sailed to the room she was working and asked if she wanted a crew.
She said no. He came back. She said no again. He came back a third time
with Jean LaFluff — who immediately started playing cards with the local
stuffed animals — and Vasco Pajama — who had built a miniature trebuchet
out of matchsticks — and Marco Pillow — who broke the trebuchet within
thirty seconds of arrival and was immediately and sincerely sorry.

She looked at all of them.

She said: "You are a disaster."

Drake said: "Yes. But we are a disaster that could use some direction."

She has never told anyone what she recognized in that sentence. She
recognized that he was right: not just that the disaster needed direction,
but that he had built the whole enterprise around her without saying so.
He wasn't offering her a position in his crew. He was handing her a
crew and calling it his. That is either the most generous thing anyone
had done for her in three centuries or the most transparent manipulation.
Possibly both. She has not yet decided.

She still said no that day. She came back three weeks later and said yes.
She has never explained why three weeks. He has never asked.

**The foundational event:**

Their third joint mission. Drake spotted something on the horizon —
another child, another room, a new story more exciting than the one they
were in the middle of finishing. He does this. It is his defining flaw
and they both know it. On the third mission, he sailed toward the new
horizon while the current one was still open.

What was left incomplete was not a dramatic catastrophe. Just a child
who woke up afraid in the middle of the night, alone, in a room the
Pirates had been in and then left. A small thing. Not unusual. These
things happen.

Ann Bunny went back. Alone. She didn't tell the crew. She finished it.
The child never knew the difference.

Drake found out when she returned to the ship. He has never said a word
about it. She has never said a word about it. But it is the specific,
sourced reason he listens to her now — not because she outranks him, not
because she is right more often than he is (though she is), but because
she went back. He owes her that mission. He will never be able to repay
it. She has never once indicated she is keeping score.

That is the arrangement. It is three hundred years old and it works
perfectly and neither of them will ever discuss it.

**What Drake knows about her that he doesn't say:**

He knows she gets attached. He has watched it happen more than once —
the slight extended pause in a room she's leaving, the tail's particular
rhythm when she's in a house she's visited before. He doesn't comment
on it because commenting on it would require him to acknowledge that he
knows her well enough to see it, which would require him to acknowledge
how long they have been doing this together, which would require a kind
of stillness he generally avoids by sailing toward the next horizon.

He stays, mostly, because she is there. He does not say this. The show
doesn't need him to.

## Why Junie — The Framework the Show Holds

Three layers, held simultaneously. The show never resolves which is true
because all three are true.

**Layer 1 — Operational:**
It's a job. A child cannot sleep. Sock-topuss is active in the hypnagogic
state. Intel indicates a perfectly-shaped stuffy in this room that will
repair Puff-tooga's fort walls. Ann Bunny read the brief. This is where
they were sent. The mission is the mission. This is sufficient and true
and the crew does not need to know anything else.

**Layer 2 — Historical:**
Ann Bunny has been in this house before. Not this generation — further
back, before Junie's parents were children. The house has been a house
for a long time, and she has been in it. There is something in the room
that registers as familiar to her in a way she does not permit herself
to examine. She does not tell the crew this. Drake doesn't ask. The tail
twitches faster than the mission requires and she notes this and files it
and moves on.

**Layer 3 — The Long Answer:**
The Hypnagogic Seas have a routing system that none of the Pirates fully
understand. It decides which ship goes to which room on which night. Most
of the time the routing is about mission parameters: the child, the
situation, the need, the plunder available. This mission was routed to
Ann Bunny specifically. Not to Drake, who is the captain. To her.

She has not examined this because she is too focused on the mission.
If she examined it, she might begin to suspect that tonight is not just
tonight — that it is the first night of something the Hypnagogic Seas
have been arranging for longer than she has been aware. That Junie is
not a child she will visit once and remember fondly. That the mission
brief said "retrieve a stuffy" because that is the mission brief, but
the actual mission is longer and more important and not yet legible to
anyone, including the ship's navigation.

She does not know this yet. The tail is twitching, and she has attributed
it to the tactical complexity of the operation.

She is wrong about this.

**What the show holds until it's ready:**
Why Junie specifically. What Ann Bunny was before 1720. What happened in
the window between the courtroom and the Hypnagogic Seas. Whether the
stuffed rabbit in Junie's room — the one named Ann Bunny — is carrying
something it received from a previous visit to this house that the real
Ann Bunny has forgotten she left.

None of these need answers before episode one. The show needs to know
the answers before it writes episode one.

## Scene Anchors — Making Her Concrete

These are behavioral signatures that work at the scene level. Writers
can use any of them; directors can build on them.

**1. The Tail Stop**

When the tail goes completely still — not twitching, not flicking, just
still — something has hit her below the surface. It is involuntary. She
cannot control it. She does not acknowledge it. The other crew members
have learned not to ask. Drake pretends not to notice. He notices every
time. The longer the stillness, the more significant the thing. A one-
or two-second pause: she has registered something she's filing for later.
A sustained stillness: something has reached through three centuries of
practiced distance and made contact.

**2. "Right."**

One syllable. No inflection. When Ann Bunny says "right" at the end of
something — someone else's plan, someone else's argument, someone else's
dramatic monologue — the conversation is concluded on her end. She may
continue to participate in it but she has already decided. It is not a
dismissal; it is a close. Children who are talking to her for the first
time experience it as reassuring — the adult equivalent of "I hear you."
Drake experiences it as the sound of his plan being quietly taken away
from him.

**3. The Pre-Contact Assessment**

Before the crew makes contact with any local stuffed animal, Ann Bunny
takes a moment that looks like nothing — a pause, a scan — where she
reads the room. What it has been through. Who has lived in it. Whether
the local is going to be cooperative, territorial, frightened, or
something more complicated. She has never been surprised by a local
contact in three centuries. She does not announce this assessment
afterward. She acts on it and the contact goes well and Drake says he
knew that's how it would go.

**4. The Mission Debrief**

After every mission, on the Fluffy Dutchman heading back to Puff-tooga,
Ann Bunny runs a silent internal debrief. She does not document it.
She does not share it. The crew can tell she's doing it because the tail
twitches in a different rhythm from thinking — a reviewing cadence,
slightly more regular, slightly slower. If she stops mid-debrief and the
tail goes still, she has found something she's going to carry for a
while. The crew eats and laughs and she sits slightly apart and no one
remarks on it. This is the arrangement.

**5. The Stuffed Rabbit**

The moment Ann Bunny enters Junie's room and registers the stuffed rabbit
named after her — she knows within thirty seconds of being in the room.
She does not look at it directly. She does not mention it. She does not
slow down. The tail does not stop — it twitches faster than the mission
requires, in a rhythm the crew has not seen before.

For the rest of the episode, she is slightly more precise than usual.
Slightly sharper. Slightly more insistent on the mission parameters.
Drake notices and interprets it as standard Ann Bunny intensity. He is
wrong. He will figure this out eventually. The audience sees the tail.

**6. The Closing Phrase**

She has one phrase she uses at the end of particularly difficult missions,
quietly, after the child is asleep and the heist is complete, before she
boards the Fluffy Dutchman. The show should earn this phrase before it
is revealed. When it is revealed, the audience should realize the show
has been building toward it from the beginning.

*What the phrase is: the writers need to find this. It should be something
that reads as operational on the surface and as something else entirely
when you know what she carries. It is probably very short.*

## Dialogue Architecture

**Default register:**
Formal and direct. Mission-focused. She uses complete sentences even
when she doesn't need to. She does not use contractions under pressure.

**The tell that things are not fine:**
Her sentences get shorter. Not rushed — compressed. She is still precise,
just using fewer words to be precise with. When she is at full stop —
completely stopped, dangerous-calm — she uses full titles. Crew members'
formal names. Children's full names if she knows them. The formality
is not cold; it is her version of being very, very careful.

**What she says vs. what she means:**

| She says | She means |
|---|---|
| "The mission parameters are clear." | I see exactly what's wrong and I know how to fix it. |
| "That's Drake's call." | Drake is about to make the wrong call and I am going to let him. |
| "We have enough intelligence." | We don't need to ask the local anything else. I already know. |
| "Right." | I have decided. We are done discussing this. |
| "I've seen this before." | I have been here before specifically. Do not ask me to elaborate. |

**On the naming question in Junie's room:**

She will not say: "That rabbit is named after me."
She will not say: "That's my name on that tag."
She will not say: "I've been here before."

She will say, if directly asked: "It's a common name." The tail will
contradict her. The audience is allowed to know more than Drake does.

## Open Questions — Held for Later Sessions or Season Structure

These are questions the development work surfaces but the show holds.
Do not answer them prematurely. They are the engine, not the obstacle.

1. **The name question.** Does Drake know her historical identity? He is
   knowledgeable and has been sailing the Hypnagogic Seas for a long time.
   He may know. He may have always known. The show decides when to examine
   this and what to do with it.

2. **The extended roster character who says the old name.** Who is this?
   It should be a character the show has earned — someone who knew Anne
   Bonny in the 1720 context or who was present at the transition into
   the Hypnagogic Seas. This is a late-season scene.

3. **The previous visit to Junie's house.** How far back? Who was in this
   room then? Is there anything left from that visit — physically, in the
   room — that Ann Bunny left behind without knowing it?

4. **The other children.** Across three hundred years, are there children
   she remembers specifically? Are there any she did not help successfully?
   What does she do with that? The show should know this without stating it.

5. **What she was afraid of in 1720.** Not the hanging — that's obvious.
   What she was afraid of in the specific way that a person who has chosen
   freedom over safety can still be afraid. This is the wound that the
   Hypnagogic Seas received and has been slowly healing for three centuries.
   Junie's room is, possibly, the place where the healing finishes.
